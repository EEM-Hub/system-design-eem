## Ledgers in Accounting

A ledger is the principal book of accounts in which financial transactions are permanently recorded. It serves as the central repository where entries from supporting journals (day books) are posted, summarized, and used to generate financial statements at the end of an accounting period.

## Key Concepts

- A ledger is a book or collection of accounts recording accounting transactions
- Each account contains three elements: an opening (brought-forward) balance, a list of transactions recorded as debits or credits in separate columns, and a closing (carry-forward) balance
- Transactions flow from journals to ledgers — journals record individual transactions by date, ledgers provide permanent summaries
- **Fundamental rule**: for every debit recorded, there must be a corresponding credit (total debits must equal total credits)
- Three main types of ledgers:
  - **Sales ledger** (debtors ledger) — records accounts receivable; tracks what customers owe the business
  - **Purchase ledger** (creditors ledger) — records accounts payable; tracks what the business owes suppliers
  - **General ledger** — contains the five main account types: assets, liabilities, income, expenses, and equity
- Journals may be totaled monthly and posted to ledgers depending on company procedures
- Financial statements are generated from summary totals in the ledgers at period end

## Commands and Syntax

Not applicable — this is a conceptual accounting topic, not a technical system. However, the logical structure of a ledger account follows this pattern:

```
Account Name
─────────────────────────────────────
Opening Balance: $X
  Date | Description | Debit | Credit
  ──────────────────────────────────
  ...  | ...         | ...   | ...
Closing Balance: $Y
```

## Relationships

- **Journals (day books)** — source documents from which transactions are posted into ledgers
- **Debits and credits** — the dual-entry mechanism used within ledger accounts
- **Financial statements** — generated from ledger summary totals at period end
- **Bookkeeping** — the broader discipline that governs ledger maintenance
- **Distributed ledger** — a modern extension of the concept where a consensus-based, replicated ledger is shared and synchronized across multiple geographic locations (foundational concept for blockchain)

## Exam-Relevant Points

- Know the three ledger types and what each records: sales ledger (receivables), purchase ledger (payables), general ledger (assets, liabilities, income, expenses, equity)
- The five main account types in the general ledger: assets, liabilities, income, expenses, equity
- The double-entry principle: every debit must have a corresponding credit
- The flow of data: individual transactions are recorded in journals first, then posted (individually or as totals) to ledgers
- Ledgers are permanent summaries; journals are chronological records of individual transactions
- Financial statements are derived from ledger totals, not directly from journals
